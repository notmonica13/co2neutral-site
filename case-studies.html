Case Study: Optimizing Injection Moulding Costs for Automotive Panels

08.09.2025

Injection-moulded plastic parts are critical in vehicle interiors and exterior trim, yet tooling and production costs can spiral when design complexity isn’t controlled. This case study examines how a cost engineering team used detailed should‑cost models and collaborative design-to-cost reviews to reduce tooling costs and accelerate quotation cycles.

Identifying the real cost drivers

The company’s cost engineering team discovered that traditional supplier quotes didn’t provide enough transparency. Using aPriori’s CAD-integrated cost models, they identified tooling drivers such as steel grades, rough cutting, mould finish and gate type, highlighting where design complexity was adding unnecessary expense【296027719082906†L245-L255】.

Design-to-cost changes

Armed with detailed cost breakdowns, engineers worked with sourcing teams to modify parts. For example, replacing circular armrest holes with simple slots reduced cycle time and simplified tooling【296027719082906†L281-L299】.

Results and lessons learned

  • Granular should-cost models cut quotation cycles from weeks to hours and enabled fact‑based negotiations【296027719082906†L242-L274】.
  • Comparisons of global manufacturing rates (Europe vs North America vs Asia) helped sourcing teams choose the most cost-effective region【296027719082906†L242-L274】.
  • Design changes delivered savings of up to 30% on tooling costs or millions of euros【296027719082906†L281-L299】.

This case illustrates how a structured cost-engineering approach — combining detailed cost models, design-for-manufacture principles and cross-functional collaboration — can produce significant cost and schedule improvements.
